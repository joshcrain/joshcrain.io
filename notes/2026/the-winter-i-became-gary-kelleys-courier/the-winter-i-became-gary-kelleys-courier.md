title: The Winter I Became Gary Kelley’s Courier

In the winter of 2009, I received an email from someone at [Graphis Magazine](https://graphis.com/). She had tried unsuccessfully to reach [Gary Kelley](https://www.garykelleystudio.com/) by email and wondered if I could help. My office was just a few blocks from Gary’s studio. As a young designer I was thrilled. 

The agency I worked at had done a small amount of work for him—nothing significant—but because of proximity (and maybe a bit of luck), I found myself helping connect someone in New York with an internationally recognized, Iowa-based illustrator who didn’t use email.

She had a message and a PDF she wanted to pass along. So I printed everything, put it in a manila envelope with a short note, and walked it over to Main Street. On the way, I stopped for a quesadilla at my favorite Mexican grill, then climbed the stairs to his studio.

The space was full of work—canvases, materials, the accumulation of a life spent making things. He was on the phone when I walked in, sitting at a small desk mid-conversation. I quietly explained why I was there. He nodded, understood immediately, and gave a thumbs up as I handed him the envelope.

Even then, it felt like a bit of a novelty—no inbox, no constant reachability, no pressure to be available at all times. Gary was focused on the work itself.

It’s easy to lose sight of what matters. We tend to think that the more ways we have to communicate, the more our voice will be amplified—but often the opposite is true.

I was saddened to hear this week of Gary Kelley’s passing. He was someone wholly focused on his work and stayed true to his own voice.
